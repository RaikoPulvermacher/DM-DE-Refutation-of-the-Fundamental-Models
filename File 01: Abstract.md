# Abstract

This project emerged from the theoretical development of the model Tensor of Realities (TdR)
(https://doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.18727529), in which the relationship between perception, light, and
spatial structure was re-examined. During the elaboration of the TdR it became clear that light is
not the fundamental level of description of reality, but is itself already a derived phenomenon that
is only perceivable within a pre-existing spatial structure. Spatial structure is therefore logically
and physically prior to light and determines how light propagates and how observers interpret
measurement data.

A central result of this work is the recognition that modern cosmology, while correctly treating the
speed of light as locally invariant, incorrectly extends this local invariance to a globally
constant observable quantity. This generalisation is unjustified and leads to systematic errors in
all models based on global homogeneity, averaging, and the cosmological Doppler effect.

A thought experiment, analogous to Einstein's famous "riding alongside the photon" experiment, but
from the perspective of the photon itself, showed that the perception of a photon depends entirely
on the local spatial structure. In regions of low gravity (voids) the metric is stretched, leading
to effectively higher light travel speeds (e.g. ~3.3c), while in dense regions (clusters) the
metric is compressed and the effective light travel time appears shorter. The local speed of light
remains invariant, but the global observation is structurally variable.

Redshift and blueshift remain unchanged as measured values, but all quantities derived from them –
velocity, distance, expansion, density – depend on the false assumption of a global c. As a result,
the Hubble parameter, the Friedmann equations, and the ΛCDM model are systematically distorted. The
phenomena "Dark Matter" and "Dark Energy" arise as interpretation artefacts from homogenisation,
averaging, and the inadmissible transfer of local relativity to global scales.

In addition, the analysis of local gravitational dynamics shows that nearby galaxies can exhibit
redshifts that arise not from cosmological expansion but from gravitational acceleration processes.
Analogous to gravitational slingshot manoeuvres in spaceflight, galaxies can be accelerated and
"catapulted" through interaction with massive clusters or filaments. These processes generate
redshifts that appear identical to those interpreted today as "accelerated expansion", but are of
purely local-dynamic origin.

Since humanity has been observing the universe for only a few centuries – a negligible period
compared to cosmic timescales – dynamic processes such as gravitational catapult mechanisms,
resonance cycles, and structural metric changes are not captured. The assumption that observed
redshifts are exclusively attributable to expansion is therefore unjustified and leads to systematic
misinterpretations.

By separating local and global c, accounting for structural metric variation, and applying the TdR
model, real density distributions and gravitational fields can be reconstructed directly from
existing measurement data – without additional hypothetical components. This project thereby provides
a consistent theoretical framework that fundamentally corrects modern cosmology without violating
established physics.
