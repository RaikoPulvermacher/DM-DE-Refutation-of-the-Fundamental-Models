# Error Analysis of the Friedmann Equations

## 1. Introduction
The Friedmann equations form the mathematical foundation of modern cosmology. They
describe the temporal evolution of the cosmic scale factors under the assumption of
a homogeneous and isotropic universe. Although the equations are mathematically
correct, they are applied in cosmology to a universe that does not fulfil these
prerequisites. This gives rise to systematic misinterpretations that directly lead
to the concepts "Dark Matter" and "Dark Energy".

---

## 2. Friedmann Is Based on Averages
The Friedmann equations use:

- averaged densities  
- averaged energies  
- averaged curvatures  
- averaged metrics  

These averages assume:

- that local structures play no role  
- that voids and clusters "average out" each other  
- that spacetime is smooth on large scales  

In reality, however, the universe is:

- extremely inhomogeneous  
- structured in voids, filaments, and clusters  
- metrically variable  
- anisotropic  

The averages are therefore physically meaningless.  
An equation based on averages cannot correctly describe local or structural effects.

---

## 3. Friedmann Assumes Homogeneity
The central prerequisite of the Friedmann equations is the cosmological principle:

$$
\text{The universe is homogeneous and isotropic.}
$$

This assumption is empirically incorrect:

- Voids have stretched metric → $c_{\text{eff}} > c$  
- Clusters have compressed metric → $c_{\text{eff}} < c$  
- Filaments generate anisotropic metric  
- Local gravitational fields dominate the structure  
- The matter distribution is fractal up to hundreds of Mpc  

If the metric is not homogeneous, the scale factor $a(t)$ cannot be globally defined.

The Friedmann equations thereby lose their physical meaning.

---

## 4. Friedmann Generates DM/DE as Mathematical Gap-Fillers
When the Friedmann equations are applied to an inhomogeneous universe, discrepancies
inevitably arise between:

- observed gravitation  
- calculated gravitation  
- observed redshift  
- calculated redshift  

In the standard model these discrepancies are "fixed" by two hypothetical components:

- **Dark Matter** (to explain missing gravitation)  
- **Dark Energy** (to explain apparent acceleration)

Both arise not from observations but from the incorrect application of the Friedmann
equations.

Formally:

$$
\text{Error in homogeneity assumption} \Rightarrow \text{Error in energy budget} \Rightarrow \text{Introduction of DM/DE}
$$

DM/DE are mathematical gap-fillers, not physical discoveries.

---

## 5. Friedmann Ignores Structural Metric Variation
The equations assume:

$$
ds^2 = -c^2 dt^2 + a(t)^2 \, d\Sigma^2
$$

This implies:

- a single global scale factor  
- a single global time  
- a single global metric  

In reality, however, there exist:

- local times (gravitational time dilation)  
- local metrics (void vs. cluster)  
- local light travel times  
- local effective velocities  

The Friedmann metric is therefore not applicable.

---

## 6. Consequences for Cosmology
If the Friedmann equations are not applicable to the real universe, the following
follow:

- no global expansion  
- no accelerated expansion  
- no Dark Energy  
- no Dark Matter  
- no global scale factor  
- no ΛCDM model  

All of modern cosmology is based on an equation whose prerequisites are not met.

---

## 7. Conclusion
The Friedmann equations are mathematically correct but physically misapplied. They
assume homogeneity, isotropy, and global metric – conditions that the real universe
does not fulfil. The resulting discrepancies are interpreted as "Dark Matter" and
"Dark Energy", even though they are merely artefacts of an inadmissible model
assumption. A structural, local treatment of spacetime renders these hypothetical
components superfluous.
