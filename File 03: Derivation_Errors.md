# Derivation Errors in Modern Cosmology

## 1. Introduction
Redshift $z$ and blueshift are direct, experimentally robust measured values.
They arise from changes in the wavelength of light, regardless of how these
changes are interpreted. The measurement itself is correct. Errors arise only when
physical quantities such as velocity, distance, or expansion are derived from
these measured values.

Modern cosmology assumes that the speed of light is globally constant as an
observable, and that the universe is homogeneous. Both assumptions are unjustified
and lead to systematic misinterpretations.

---

## 2. Redshift Remains Unchanged
Redshift $z$ is defined as:

$$
z = \frac{\lambda_{\text{observed}} - \lambda_{\text{emitted}}}{\lambda_{\text{emitted}}}
$$

This quantity is:

- directly measurable  
- model-independent  
- independent of interpretation  
- independent of cosmological assumptions  

The measurement is correct.  
The interpretation is incorrect.

---

## 3. Blueshift Remains Unchanged
Blueshift is also a pure measured value:

- it indicates shorter wavelengths  
- it occurs in gravitational potentials  
- it occurs during local approach  
- it is independent of cosmological models  

The measurement is correct.  
The interpretation is incorrect.

---

## 4. Why Velocity ≠ z
Modern cosmology interprets redshift as the Doppler effect:

$$
v \approx z \cdot c
$$

This equation is only locally valid, not globally.

Reasons:

- The Doppler effect assumes a flat metric.  
- Cosmological structures (voids, filaments, clusters) alter the metric.  
- $c_{\text{eff}}$ is structurally variable.  
- Gravitational potentials generate redshift without motion.  
- Gravitational slingshot effects generate redshift without expansion.

Consequence:

$$
z \ \text{does not measure velocity, but metric variation}
$$

---

## 5. Why Distance ≠ z
Standard cosmology uses redshift as a distance measure:

$$
D \approx \frac{c}{H_0} \cdot z
$$

This equation is incorrect because:

- it assumes global $c$  
- it assumes homogeneity  
- it assumes constant metric  
- it does not account for structural differences  

In a universe with:

- Voids (stretched metric)  
- Clusters (compressed metric)  
- Filaments (anisotropic metric)  

distance is not proportional to $z$.

Consequence:

$$
D \neq f(z)
$$

---

## 6. Why Expansion ≠ z
Modern cosmology assumes:

$$
z \Rightarrow \text{Expansion}
$$

This is incorrect.

Redshift arises from:

- gravitational potentials  
- metric variation  
- structural differences  
- local acceleration processes (gravitational slingshot)  
- different light travel times  
- variable effective speed of light $c_{\text{eff}}$

None of these effects has anything to do with expansion.

Consequence:

$$
z \ \text{is not evidence for expansion}
$$

---

## 7. Why DM/DE Arise from Derivation Errors
When velocity, distance, and expansion are incorrectly derived from $z$, the result is:

- incorrect density parameters  
- incorrect velocities  
- incorrect accelerations  
- incorrect energy budgets  

Modern cosmology "fixes" these errors through:

- Dark Matter (to explain missing gravitation)  
- Dark Energy (to explain apparent acceleration)

Both are artefacts of an incorrect derivation chain:

$$
z \rightarrow v \rightarrow D \rightarrow H_0 \rightarrow \Omega_m, \Omega_\Lambda
$$

If the first step is incorrect, all subsequent steps are incorrect.

---

## 8. Conclusion
Redshift and blueshift are correct measured values, but their interpretation within the framework of
standard cosmology is flawed. The derivation of velocity, distance, and expansion from $z$ assumes
global homogeneity and constant global $c$ – assumptions that are physically untenable. The concepts
"Dark Matter" and "Dark Energy" arise as a direct consequence of these derivation errors and
disappear as soon as the structural variability of spacetime is correctly accounted for.
