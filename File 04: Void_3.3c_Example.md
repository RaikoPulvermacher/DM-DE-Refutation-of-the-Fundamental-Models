# Void Effect: Why Voids Show Effective Speeds > c

## 1. Introduction
Voids are large-scale regions of extremely low matter density. They contain
almost no galaxies, almost no gas, and only minimal gravitational potentials.
In such regions the spacetime metric is less curved and therefore structurally
"stretched". This metric dilation causes light to appear to travel effectively
faster over large distances, even though the local speed of light $c_{\text{local}}$
remains unchanged.

This chapter shows why voids can produce effective speeds of light of approximately
$3.3c$ and why this does not represent a violation of the theory of relativity.

---

## 2. Metric Dilation in Voids
In voids gravitation is minimal. As a result:

- spacetime is less curved  
- metric distances between points are larger  
- light travel time per metric unit is shorter  
- the effective speed of light increases  

Formally:

$$
c_{\text{eff}} = \frac{\text{metric distance}}{\text{light travel time}}
$$

When the metric distance increases due to metric dilation while the light travel
time remains approximately the same, the result is:

$$
c_{\text{eff}} > c_{\text{local}}
$$

This is not a physical violation, but a direct consequence of the metric structure.

## 3. Lower Gravitation → Shorter Effective Travel Times
In voids the gravitational potential is so minimal that:

- photons are less "slowed down"  
- the Shapiro delay is minimal  
- spacetime appears nearly flat  
- light travel time per unit path decreases
