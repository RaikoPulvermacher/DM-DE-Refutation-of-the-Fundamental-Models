# Local c vs. Global c

## 1. Introduction
Modern physics treats the speed of light $c$ as locally invariant.
This local invariance is experimentally confirmed and forms the basis of
Special and General Relativity. Cosmology, however, extends this
local invariance to a globally constant observable quantity. This
generalisation is unjustified and leads to systematic errors in all
models based on global homogeneity, averaging, and the cosmological
Doppler effect.

This document describes the fundamental distinction between local
$c_{\text{local}}$ and globally or structurally effective
$c_{\text{eff}}$, and shows why this distinction is crucial for the
correct interpretation of cosmological measurement data.

---

## 2. Definition of $c_{\text{local}}$
The local speed of light is defined as:

$$
c_{\text{local}} = 299\,792\,458 \ \text{m/s}
$$

Properties:

- invariant in every local inertial frame  
- independent of observer, direction, or frequency  
- basis of the theory of relativity  
- experimentally confirmed (Michelson–Morley, GPS, laser interferometry)

This quantity describes exclusively the propagation of light **within a
local spacetime region** whose metric is sufficiently flat.

---

## 3. Definition of $c_{\text{eff}}$ (structurally effective)
The effective speed of light $c_{\text{eff}}$ describes the
observed light travel time over large cosmological distances and depends on the
spatial structure:

$$
c_{\text{eff}} = \frac{\text{distance}}{\text{light travel time}}
$$

Properties:

- dependent on the local metric along the entire light path  
- dependent on gravitation, density, and structure (void, filament, cluster)  
- can appear greater or smaller than $c_{\text{local}}$  
- is **not a violation of relativity**, but a consequence of the metric

Example:

- In voids the metric is stretched → light travels "faster" → $c_{\text{eff}} > c$  
- In clusters the metric is compressed → light travels "slower" → $c_{\text{eff}} < c$

---

## 4. Examples: Void and Cluster

### Void (low density)
- minimal gravitation  
- stretched metric  
- lower curvature  
- shorter effective travel time  
- observed $c_{\text{eff}} \approx 3.3c$

Consequence:  
Redshift arises not from expansion but from metric variation.

### Cluster (high density)
- strong gravitation  
- compressed metric  
- higher curvature  
- longer effective travel time  
- observed $c_{\text{eff}} < c$

Consequence:  
Blueshift arises not from approach but from gravitational potential.

---

## 5. Why the Measurement Stays the Same but the Interpretation Does Not
Redshift and blueshift $z$ are **direct measured values**:

- spectral lines shift  
- this shift is model-independent  
- the measurement is correct

What is incorrect:

- the derivation of velocity from $z$  
- the derivation of distance from $z$  
- the derivation of expansion from $z$  
- the derivation of density from $z$

Reason:

$$
z \ \text{measures the metric, not the velocity}
$$

Modern cosmology, however, interprets $z$ as the Doppler effect, which is only
locally correct, not globally.

---

## 6. Why Global Homogeneity Is Incorrect
The assumption of a homogeneous universe leads to:

- averaging over inhomogeneous structures  
- incorrect distance scales  
- incorrect velocities  
- incorrect density parameters  
- the artificial introduction of Dark Matter and Dark Energy

The universe is, however:

- structured  
- inhomogeneous  
- anisotropic  
- metrically variable

The global homogeneity assumption is therefore physically unjustified and
mathematically incorrect.

---

## 7. Conclusion
The distinction between local $c_{\text{local}}$ and structurally
effective $c_{\text{eff}}$ is crucial for the correct interpretation
of cosmological measurement data. Modern cosmology ignores this distinction
and thereby generates systematic misinterpretations that lead to the concepts
"Dark Matter" and "Dark Energy". A correct treatment of spatial structure
eliminates these artefacts and enables a physically consistent description
of the universe.
